Basic View

This is a sheet of atoms connected by tiny springs. When one atom moves, it pulls neighbors, so waves run through the lattice.

The color of each bond shows stretching. Gray means near-rest length. Warmer tones mean stronger stretch.

You can drag near an atom to disturb the sheet and watch vibrations spread out.

Spring idea: \(F = kx\). Bigger \(k\) means stiffer bonds and faster oscillations.

First-Year Physics View

Each atom obeys Newton's law with damping:

\[m\ddot{\mathbf r}_i = \sum_j \mathbf F_{ij} - \gamma \dot{\mathbf r}_i + \mathbf F_{\text{drag}}\]

Nearest-neighbor bonds are harmonic by default:

\[U_{ij} = \tfrac12 k (r_{ij}-L_0)^2, \quad \mathbf F_{ij} = -\nabla_i U_{ij}\]

Numerical integration uses time step \(dt\). Larger \(dt\) runs faster but can become unstable.

Advanced View

The model mixes first-neighbor bond forces and optional second-neighbor angular/lattice stiffening terms.

Optional Morse bonds use:

\[U_M(r)=D_e\left(1-e^{-\alpha(r-r_e)}\right)^2 - D_e\]

This introduces anharmonicity relevant for large strain and can support bond-breaking/reformation thresholds \(f_{\text{break}}, f_{\text{reform}}\).

For small displacements, normal modes approximate acoustic/optical branches of a two-atom basis honeycomb lattice.

Things to Try

  1. Set damping \(\gamma\) near zero and pluck one corner; observe long-lived oscillations.
  2. Increase \(k\) from 20 to 40 and compare wave speed visually.
  3. Turn on Morse potential and raise initial offset; look for asymmetric restoring motion.
  4. Enable bond breaking, increase perturbation, and find threshold where defects persist.
  5. Lower steps per frame and compare numerical smoothness versus performance.
  6. Use recording at different FPS and compare final video fluidity.

Quiz

Answer the questions, then press Reveal answers.
1. Increasing \(k\) mostly does what?
Correct answer: B. Higher \(k\) means larger restoring force \(F=kx\), so oscillation frequency rises.
2. What is the role of \(\gamma\)?
Correct answer: B. The damping term is \(\mathbf F_d=-\gamma\mathbf v\), which removes kinetic energy.
3. If \(dt\) is too large, what can happen?
Correct answer: C. Too-large \(dt\) can violate stability constraints of explicit integration.
4. Bond color primarily visualizes:
Correct answer: A. Color encodes strain \(\varepsilon=(r-L_0)/L_0\) with scaling and saturation controls.
5. Morse potential is useful because it:
Correct answer: B. Morse potential becomes asymmetric away from equilibrium.
6. How does the number of atoms scale with unit cells \(n\)?
Correct answer: B. There are two atoms per unit cell, so \(N=2n^2\).
7. Increasing steps per frame mainly changes:
Correct answer: A. \(\Delta t_{frame}=\text{spf}\cdot dt\), so more dynamics are advanced each visual frame.
8. Bond breaking triggers when:
Correct answer: C. A bond is removed when stretched beyond the break threshold.
9. Angle stiffness contributes by:
Correct answer: A. It augments elasticity through non-nearest-neighbor coupling.
10. Why use smaller \(dt\) with large \(k\)?
Correct answer: A. Stiffer systems have higher natural frequencies, demanding smaller timesteps.
